Hi, I am Sanitation Worker Tuhina from Queens. We start off our shift by clocking-in at our local garage then set out to pick up your household trash or recyclables.
Once trash has been collected from the sidewalk, our Sanitation Workers make their way to the North Shore Marine Transfer Station.
Before entering, the collection truck is weighed so we can determine the load’s tonnage. It is also scanned for radiation. If it tests positive, the truck is removed to ensure the protection of our Sanitation Workers.
After weighing, a DSNY employee will be on the tipping floor to direct truck drivers to available tipping bays.
Trash from the DSNY truck will land on the loading floor located below the elevated tipping bay. Two front-end loaders push the trash into an available container, where it is then compressed by an excavator.
Once the container is full, Sanitation Workers on the lidding floor seal the container.
From here on, the container is the responsibility of a private contractor, who loads it onto a barge with a crane.
The barge––and containers full of trash––is guided by a tugboat to a container terminal, where it is off-loaded and placed on a train.

Hi, I am Sanitation Worker Tom from Manhattan. We start off our shift by clocking-in at our local garage then set out to pick up your household trash or recyclables.
After leaving our Manhattan Spring Street garage, our Sanitation Workers pick up your cardboard and paper recycling from the sidewalk, and throw it into a DSNY collection truck.
When our Sanitation Workers have completed their routes, they make their way to the 59th Street Manhattan transfer station.
Before entering, the collection truck is weighed so we can determine the load’s tonnage. It is also scanned for radiation. If it tests positive, the truck is removed to ensure the protection of our Sanitation Workers.
Then they are signaled to release their load of paper and cardboard trash directly onto a barge.
The barge––full of NYC’s paper and cardboard recyclables––is guided by a tugboat from the Upper Bay through Kill Van Kull and to Arthur Kill Narrows to the Pratt Paper Mill in Staten Island.
When the barge arrives at the Pratt Industries Paper Mill, an operator steers a giant crane claw to scoop up paper recycling from the barge.
Once at Pratt Paper Mill, the paper and cardboard recycling is first dumped into a pit and then fed into a pulper. In the pulper, the paper is blended with water to create a pulp. This pulp passes through screens to remove contaminants.
Then it is pressed, and ultimately dried and made into a large roll of recycled paper.
These huge rolls of recycled paper––which weigh 25 tons each–– are transported by a mechanized system controlled by a Mill Worker to the “Winder.”
There, the recycled paper is cut down into smaller rolls, which are then transported by a forklift driver to a storage area.
This recycled paper is ready to be distributed to a corrugator, where the rolls of paper are made into sheets of cardboard and then cut and folded into boxes.
Mill workers examine the packets of cardboard boxes on the assembly line for quality control, and if they are happy with the standard then the recycled cardboard is distributed for use across NYC.
